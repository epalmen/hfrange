HF Range Tracker — Technical Guide

How to automatically measure the propagation range of an HF station using distributed KiwiSDR receivers, FFT-based tone detection, and IC-7300 remote control.

📡 Icom IC-7300 🌍 Amsterdam, JO22 📻 40m / 20m / 10m SSB 🔬 Signal: 1000 Hz tone

1. Concept

The HF Range Tracker answers one question: how far does my signal reach? It does this by transmitting a precise audio tone from the IC-7300, then automatically connecting to public online receivers (KiwiSDRs) located at different distances around Europe and the world, measuring whether — and how well — the tone is received at each location.

One-sentence summary
The software controls your radio, generates a test tone, then "listens" on dozens of internet-connected receivers simultaneously to map where your signal actually goes.

Why not just call a friend?

A human report ("I hear you 5/9 in Berlin") gives one data point and depends on someone being available. The tracker gives you 20+ data points in one pass, automatically, any time of day, with repeatable RSSI and SNR figures. It also shows you propagation changes over time if you run it repeatedly.

2. HF Radio Propagation

How HF signals travel

At frequencies between roughly 3 and 30 MHz, radio waves can reflect off the ionosphere — a layer of ionised gas at 80–400 km altitude — and return to Earth thousands of kilometres away. This is called sky-wave propagation, and it is what makes intercontinental radio communication possible without satellites.

The skip zone — why nearby doesn't work

Between the TX antenna and the point where the ionospheric reflection lands, there is a region where the sky-wave does NOT arrive. Stations within this zone can only receive the weak ground wave, which typically dies out within 50–100 km on HF. This means a KiwiSDR in Rotterdam cannot hear a 14 MHz signal from Amsterdam via sky-wave, even though it is only 70 km away.

Ionosphere changes constantly
Solar activity (sunspot cycle), time of day, and season all shift the ionosphere. The tracker re-estimates the skip zone at each scan run based on frequency and UTC time. Run scans at different times to see how propagation changes.

Multi-hop propagation

Signals can bounce multiple times between ionosphere and Earth. On 20m and 10m, two or three hops can carry a signal from Amsterdam to North America, Australia, or Japan. Each hop adds loss but also multiplies reach. The tracker captures all of this — if a KiwiSDR in the USA hears you, it shows up green on the map.

3. How Much Power to Use

The IC-7300 can output up to 100 W. For range testing, more power is not always better — it can mask weak propagation paths and makes it harder to benchmark your antenna. Below is a practical guide:

PowerBandTypical reachWhen to use
10 W 10m 500–10 000 km (band open) Good baseline — shows true antenna performance
25 W 20m 2 000–12 000 km ★ Recommended starting point for all bands
50 W 40m / 20m 800–8 000 km Good for marginal propagation days
100 W All Maximum reach Reserve for specific tests (compare with 25 W result)
★ Recommendation: Start at 25 W
  • Plenty of power to reach across Europe on 40m and worldwide on 20m
  • Keeps ALC stable — a single sine tone at full power can stress the finals
  • Leaves a clear 6 dB headroom margin to compare antenna improvements
  • Good neighbour practice — brief test tones at 25 W cause minimal QRM
  • Set on IC-7300: Menu → SET → TX/RX → RF Power
Important: single-tone ALC warning
A steady sine wave puts full sustained power into the final stage, unlike voice which averages much lower. The IC-7300 handles this fine, but keep individual transmissions short (the tracker limits each to 10 seconds by default). Never run a continuous tone test for more than 30 seconds at 100 W.

Power vs. SNR at the receiver

Every doubling of power adds only +3 dB of SNR at the receiver. The ionospheric path loss and noise floor matter far more than a few extra watts. If the tracker shows you are not being heard, the answer is usually better antenna + better timing, not more power.

5. Why a Tone — and How Detection Works

SSB + a single audio tone

On SSB (Single SideBand), the transmitter suppresses the carrier and transmits only one sideband of the modulated audio. If you feed a pure 1000 Hz sine wave into the microphone input:

  • On USB at 14.200 MHz → RF energy appears at 14.201 MHz
  • No other frequency components are transmitted — it is a single narrow spike
  • On a receiving SDR tuned to 14.200 MHz USB, the tone arrives as audio at 1000 Hz
Why a tone beats voice
Voice is random — peak levels vary, there are gaps between words, and automated detection is unreliable. A pure sine tone is deterministic: the FFT finds a sharp peak at exactly the expected frequency every time, even when the signal is barely above the noise. This is identical to how SSB transmitters are calibrated in professional settings (IEC 60268, "single-tone test").

FFT detection step by step

  1. Audio capture: Connect to the KiwiSDR via WebSocket. Tune to our dial frequency in USB mode. Capture 12 seconds of raw 16-bit PCM audio at 12 000 Hz sample rate (= 144 000 samples).
  2. Windowing: Apply a Hann window to reduce spectral leakage (prevents energy from our 1000 Hz tone from spilling into adjacent bins).
  3. FFT: Compute the magnitude spectrum with numpy.fft.rfft(). With 144 000 samples at 12 kHz, each FFT bin is about 0.08 Hz wide — very precise tone location.
  4. Tone power: Find the maximum magnitude in a ±50 Hz window around 1000 Hz. Convert to dB.
  5. Noise floor: Compute the median magnitude of all bins between 200 Hz and 5000 Hz, excluding the tone window. Convert to dB.
  6. SNR decision: SNR = tone_power − noise_floor. If SNR ≥ 10 dB → HEARD ✓. Below 10 dB → not heard.

6. IC-7300 Control (hamlib / rigctld)

The IC-7300 uses Icom's CI-V (Computer Interface V) protocol — a serial bus that lets software control frequency, mode, PTT and read meters. Over USB, the IC-7300 presents a virtual COM port (CP210x driver on Windows).

hamlib + rigctld

hamlib is the universal radio control library (supports 600+ rigs). We run it in daemon mode (rigctld) which exposes a simple TCP socket: 

rigctld -m 3073 -r COM3 -s 19200 -P RIG -t 4532
        │       │       │        └─ TCP port our software connects to
        │       │       └─ baud rate (CI-V over USB: 19200)
        │       └─ Windows COM port for the IC-7300
        └─ hamlib model number 3073 = Icom IC-7300

Our radio_control.py connects to localhost:4532 and sends plain-text commands:

CommandAction
F 14200000Set VFO to 14.200 MHz
M USB 0Set mode to USB, default passband
T 1 / T 0PTT on / PTT off
l STRENGTHRead S-meter
l SWRRead SWR meter

USB audio (tone injection)

The IC-7300 also presents a USB audio codec (separate from the serial port). When in USB-D (data) mode, audio sent to this device goes directly to the TX modulator — bypassing the microphone.

tone_generator.py uses Python's sounddevice library to stream a 1000 Hz sine wave to the IC-7300 audio device. Combined with PTT via rigctld, this creates a clean, hands-free test transmission.

IC-7300 menu setting required
Before the software can control PTT and inject audio, set on the radio:
Menu → SET → Connectors → USB SEND/MOD → USB MOD = Data / USB SEND = Data
This routes the USB audio to the TX and allows software PTT.

7. The KiwiSDR Network

KiwiSDR is an open-source software-defined radio built on a Beaglebone Black. Owners connect them to the internet and make them publicly accessible at rx.kiwisdr.com. There are over 600 receivers worldwide, many covering HF bands 0–30 MHz.

How we use them

  • Fetch the public receiver list (JSON from sdr.hu / scrape rx.kiwisdr.com)
  • Calculate great-circle distance from Amsterdam to each receiver
  • Filter to the useful range — outside the skip zone but within propagation reach
  • Connect via WebSocket and tune to our test frequency
  • Stream 12 seconds of audio, run FFT, report SNR

WebSocket audio frame format

KiwiSDR binary audio frame:
  Byte  0– 2 : "SND"                (frame tag)
  Byte  3    : flags
  Bytes 4– 7 : sequence number      (uint32 big-endian)
  Bytes 8– 9 : RSSI                 (int16 big-endian, dBm × 10)
  Bytes 10–11: GPS nanoseconds      (unused)
  Bytes 12+  : 16-bit signed PCM    (little-endian, mono, 12 000 Hz)
No KiwiSDR account needed
Public receivers allow up to 4 simultaneous connections. Our scanner connects briefly (12 s), measures, then disconnects — well within etiquette limits. The connection is identified as hfrange_pd1lvh so operators can see who is using their receiver.

8. Running the Tracker

Web UI (recommended)

  1. Start rigctld: rigctld -m 3073 -r COM3 -s 19200
  2. Start the web server: python src/web_app.py
  3. Open http://localhost:8000 in your browser
  4. Select COM port, audio device, bands → click Start scan
  5. Watch live log and map update in real time

CLI

# All bands (40m / 20m / 10m):
python src/tracker.py --config config/config.yaml --port COM3

# One band:
python src/tracker.py --band 20m --port COM3

# Listen only (no TX — useful to check for other signals first):
python src/tracker.py --band 20m --no-radio

# Loop every 30 minutes:
python src/tracker.py --loop --interval 30

Coverage map (output/map.html)

Open in any browser. Each KiwiSDR receiver is plotted at its geographical location:

  • ● Green dot — tone heard (SNR ≥ 10 dB). Click for exact RSSI and SNR.
  • ● Grey dot — not heard.
  • ★ Red star — your transmit location (Amsterdam).
  • ── Green line — propagation path where heard.

{
  "receiver": { "name": "PE4SDR Delft", "distance_km": 55.0 },
  "rssi_dbm": -95.4,
  "tone_snr_db": 21.3,
  "tone_power_db": -74.1,
  "noise_floor_db": -95.4,
  "heard": true
}

Interpreting propagation patterns

  • Ring pattern — receivers at similar distances all hear you → one clean ionospheric hop
  • One-sided — heard to the east but not west → the ionosphere is tilted or wind-shear effect
  • Nothing — band is closed, or skip zone covers all receivers in range. Try a different band or time.
  • Very far only — multi-hop. Receivers at 2× the skip distance hear you but intermediate ones don't.
